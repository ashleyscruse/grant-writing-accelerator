# Project Concept and Aims

Stage 2 ended with a "go" decision: a confirmed-eligible opportunity, a real fit, and a choice to pursue it. Stage 3 is where you turn that decision into something you can actually write. Before you draft a single section, you need two things: a sharp project concept and a set of specific aims (or goals and objectives) that map to the funder's review criteria.

This page is about the first half of Stage 3. The team comes next.

> The aims are the spine of the proposal. Reviewers read them first, score against them, and remember them. If the aims are clear, scoped, and aligned to the criteria, the rest of the proposal has a frame to hang on. If they are vague or overpromised, no amount of polish later saves it.

## Start with the one-sentence project idea

Before you write three aims, write one sentence. If you cannot say what the project does in a sentence, the aims will sprawl.

A workable format:

> This project will [do what] for [whom or what] by [the core approach], so that [the change you expect].

Examples:

| One-sentence idea |
|---|
| This project will build and validate a machine-learning method for predicting drug response in triple-negative breast cancer using patient-derived organoid data, so that researchers can prioritize candidate therapies faster. |
| This project will expand HPC and AI training capacity at an HBCU by embedding NAIRR-backed computing into three undergraduate courses, so that more students enter computational research pipelines. |

Write yours by hand first. The AI can refine it, but the core claim should be yours. You will reuse this sentence in the abstract, the aims, and the team framing, so it pays to get it tight.

## Map aims to the review criteria, not to your interests

The most common Stage 3 mistake is writing aims around what you find interesting instead of what the funder will score. Pull the solicitation's review criteria back out (you already extracted them in Stage 2) and treat them as the target.

NSF, for example, scores on Intellectual Merit and Broader Impacts. NIH scores on Significance, Innovation, Approach, and similar. A program-specific solicitation often adds its own priorities (capacity building, broadening participation, specific scientific themes). Your aims should visibly answer those.

A simple alignment table keeps you honest:

| Aim | Maps to which criterion | What it proves to a reviewer |
|---|---|---|
| Aim 1: Develop the method | Intellectual Merit / Innovation | The work is novel and technically sound |
| Aim 2: Validate against real data | Intellectual Merit / Approach | The plan is rigorous and feasible |
| Aim 3: Train students through the work | Broader Impacts | The project delivers required broader outcomes |

If an aim does not map cleanly to a criterion, ask whether it belongs in this proposal at all.

## Write objectives in SMART form

Whether the funder calls them "specific aims" (NSF, NIH) or "goals and objectives" (many federal and foundation programs), the underlying discipline is the same. A goal is the broad, long-term change you contribute to. An objective is the specific, measurable target you can hit inside the grant period.

Every objective should be SMART:

| Letter | Means | Check |
|---|---|---|
| Specific | Who, what, where | Could a stranger tell what you will do? |
| Measurable | A number, percentage, or observable indicator | Is there a metric attached? |
| Achievable | Realistic for the budget, timeline, and team | Can you defend it in front of a skeptic? |
| Relevant | Tied to the need and the funder's priorities | Does it map to a review criterion? |
| Time-bound | Has a deadline or timeframe | Is there a "by [date]"? |

Weak objective: "Increase student awareness of computational research."
SMART objective: "By month 18, embed NAIRR-backed AI modules into three undergraduate courses reaching at least 90 students per year, with 70 percent completing a hands-on computing project."

## Distinguish outputs from outcomes

Reviewers reward outcomes and discount outputs. Keep the two separate.

| Term | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Output | What the project produces or delivers | 3 course modules built; 10 workshops delivered; 1 dataset released |
| Outcome | What changes as a result | 70 percent of students demonstrate a new computational skill; 2 students enter graduate research |

A proposal that lists only outputs ("we will hold 10 workshops") reads as activity without impact. Pair each output with the outcome it drives. Save the full causal chain for the logic model in Stage 4; here you just need each aim to name what changes, not only what gets made.

## Pressure-test scope and feasibility

Once you have draft aims, stress them before a reviewer does. Two questions decide most proposals:

- Is the scope defensible in the time and budget you have? Three tight aims beat five loose ones. Reviewers punish overpromising.
- Does each aim stand on its own, or does Aim 2 collapse if Aim 1 fails? Avoid a chain where one stalled aim sinks the whole project. Independent-but-related aims are stronger.

This is where AI earns its place in Stage 3. It can structure the aims against the criteria, surface scope creep, and play the skeptical reviewer. You still choose the aims. The AI drafts and challenges; you decide.

## How AI helps (and where you stay in charge)

| AI does | You do |
|---|---|
| Drafts candidate aims from your one-sentence idea and profile | Choose which aims to keep, cut, or merge |
| Maps each aim to a named review criterion | Confirm the mapping is real, not forced |
| Flags objectives that are not measurable or time-bound | Set the actual numbers and deadlines you can defend |
| Pressure-tests scope and feasibility | Decide the scope you will commit to |
| Separates outputs from outcomes | Own which outcomes you can honestly claim |

The AI grounds every draft in your profile (`.claude/profile/`), so the aims reflect your real methods and record instead of generic filler. That is the point of Stage 1: organized context makes Stage 3 fast.

## Example prompts

Use these with the grant-writing skill once your evaluation and profile are in place. Adjust to your funder's vocabulary.

```
Draft three specific aims for this project that map to the solicitation's
review criteria, using my research profile. Keep the scope realistic for a
two-year award and label which criterion each aim addresses.
```

```
Here is my one-sentence project idea: [paste]. Turn it into a goal and three
SMART objectives. Flag any objective that is not measurable or time-bound.
```

```
Review these three aims for scope and feasibility given my team and budget.
Where am I overpromising? Which aim depends too heavily on another succeeding?
```

```
For each of these objectives, separate the output from the outcome and tell me
which ones list only outputs.
```

```
Play a skeptical NSF panel reviewer. Read these aims and tell me the three
weaknesses you would raise in the panel discussion.
```

## What "done" looks like

You finish this part of Stage 3 when you have:

- A one-sentence project idea you can defend
- Three to five specific aims or objectives, each in SMART form
- A clear mapping from each aim to a funder review criterion
- Outputs and outcomes distinguished, not blurred

With the concept and aims set, move to the team. The same opportunity needs the right people behind it, and that is the next page.
