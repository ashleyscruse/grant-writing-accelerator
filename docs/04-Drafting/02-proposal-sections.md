# Proposal Sections

This is a working reference to the main sections of a proposal. For each one, you get what the section does and the mistake that most often weakens it. Use it to know what the AI should be drafting and what you should be checking when it hands the draft back.

Not every funder asks for every section, and the headers may differ. Always mirror the funder's own section names and order. The structure below is the underlying logic, not a fixed template.

## Quick Reference

| Section | What it does | Most common mistake |
|---|---|---|
| Executive Summary / Abstract | Stands alone as a complete overview of the whole proposal | Introducing information that is not in the full proposal |
| Statement of Need | Establishes the problem with data and community voice | Centering the organization instead of the community |
| Goals, Objectives, Outcomes | Turns the need into measurable targets | Confusing outputs (what you do) with outcomes (what changes) |
| Project Description / Methodology | Explains the approach, activities, timeline, and staffing | Activities with no evidence base or no budget line |
| Logic Model | Maps inputs to activities to outputs to outcomes on one page | A broken link in the chain, or outcomes you cannot measure |
| Evaluation Plan | Shows how you will know whether it worked | Vague metrics or instruments that do not match the objectives |
| Organizational Capacity | Proves you can deliver | Claiming capacity without specific past performance |
| Sustainability Plan | Shows what continues after the grant ends | "We will seek more grants" as the only strategy |
| Budget and Budget Narrative | Justifies every dollar and ties it to the narrative | Budget math errors or lines with no narrative activity |
| Letters of Support / Partnerships | Documents real, substantive partnerships | Generic, copy-paste letters that name no specific role |

## Executive Summary / Abstract

Write this section last, position it first. It must stand alone as a complete overview: who you are, the problem and who it affects, the proposed solution and key activities, expected outcomes with specific numbers, and the total requested with the project duration. Stay under one page (often 250 words for federal grants), and use the funder's priority keywords.

**Most common mistake:** introducing information that does not appear in the full proposal. The abstract summarizes; it never adds.

## Statement of Need

Center the community, not the organization. The need belongs to the people the project will serve. Open with a specific data point or community voice, establish the problem nationally, narrow to local data specific to your service area, name root causes, show what happens if the need goes unaddressed, then transition to your solution. Combine quantitative evidence (statistics, demographics, trend lines) with qualitative evidence (survey results, direct quotes, testimony). Cite every data point with source and year, using data from the last three to five years.

Use asset-based, strengths-based language. Acknowledge community resilience and capacity. Never portray the community as broken or helpless.

**Most common mistake:** deficit-based framing that makes the community the problem instead of the people the project serves.

## Goals, Objectives, and Outcomes

Keep the four terms straight:

- **Goal:** the broad, long-term change the project contributes to. Not fully achievable within the grant period alone.
- **Objective:** a specific, measurable target achievable within the grant period.
- **Output:** what the project produces (number of trainings, people served, materials created).
- **Outcome:** what changes as a result (skills gained, behavior changed, improved indicators).

Every objective must be SMART: specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound. Delivering 10 workshops is an output. Seventy-five percent of participants demonstrating improved skills is an outcome.

**Most common mistake:** confusing outputs with outcomes, or setting objectives like "increase awareness" with no way to measure them.

## Project Description / Methodology

Lay out the approach and why you chose it, the evidence base supporting it, the activities organized by objective or phase, a timeline with milestones and responsible parties, the target population and how you will reach them, and the key personnel with their roles and time commitment. Cite the research or framework behind your approach, or, if it is innovative, explain why existing methods fall short and what evidence suggests yours will work. Address barriers to participation: transportation, language access, childcare, digital access, ADA compliance.

**Most common mistake:** describing activities that have no evidence base, or that never show up as a line in the budget.

## Logic Model

A one-page visual (table, flow chart, or diagram) that maps inputs and resources to activities, activities to outputs, outputs to short-term outcomes, and short-term outcomes to long-term impact. Every input connects to at least one activity, every activity produces at least one output, and every output links to at least one outcome. If a link is broken or missing, the model is flawed.

**Most common mistake:** skipping the short-term outcome level and jumping straight from outputs to long-term impact, or including outcomes the evaluation plan cannot actually measure.

## Evaluation Plan

Show how you will know whether the project worked. Use mixed methods: quantitative (pre/post assessments, validated instruments, administrative data, comparison groups) and qualitative (interviews, focus groups, case studies, observation). Name the specific instruments you will use, for example the USDA Household Food Security Survey Module. Include evaluation questions tied to each objective, formative and summative design, indicators and metrics, data sources, a collection timeline, analysis methods, who conducts the evaluation, and how findings get reported back to the community. Federal grants over $500K typically expect an external evaluator; whatever you choose, explain the rationale.

**Most common mistake:** vague indicators, or instruments and methods that do not actually measure the objectives you set.

## Organizational Capacity

Prove you can deliver. Demonstrate relevant experience with similar projects and populations, fiscal management and audit history, infrastructure for data collection and reporting, staff qualifications, and governance. Cite specific past projects with measurable results, especially prior grants from the same or similar funders. If the organization is new, lean on staff experience and partner capacity.

**Most common mistake:** asserting capacity in general terms without naming a specific project, result, or grant you managed.

## Sustainability Plan

Answer the funder's real question: is this a smart investment? Show how the project continues after the grant ends. Name specific revenue diversification strategies (fee-for-service, recurring donors, corporate sponsorships, earned income, government contracts, additional grants), one-time costs that will not recur, train-the-trainer models, how methods get institutionalized, and partnership commitments beyond the grant period. Make sustainability planning start at launch, not in the final year. Acknowledge honestly what may not continue and why.

**Most common mistake:** listing "seek additional grant funding" as the entire plan instead of a diversified strategy.

## Budget and Budget Narrative

Justify every dollar and tie it to the narrative. For each line item, give the detail the category requires: personnel by role, percent effort, salary basis, and fringe rate; equipment with justification and cost basis; supplies with unit cost and quantity; travel with purpose, trips, and GSA rates for federal grants; consultants with rationale and rate basis; indirect costs at the negotiated or de minimis rate. The hard rules: every narrative activity has a budget line, every budget line connects to a narrative activity, the math is error-free, and the total matches across the spreadsheet, the narrative, and the application form.

**Most common mistake:** budget math errors, or a line item that funds something the narrative never mentions (and the reverse).

## Letters of Support and Partnerships

Each letter must name the specific project, describe the partner's specific role and contribution, and ideally include data points aligned with funder priorities. Send a draft to each supporter four to six weeks before the deadline. Gather letters from diverse stakeholders: partner organizations, community leaders, elected officials, beneficiaries, subject matter experts. For formal partnerships, an MOU should name the parties, the purpose, each party's roles and resources, duration, terms, and signatures, all obtained before submission. Show partnerships are substantive: real resource sharing, joint activities, co-designed programming.

**Most common mistake:** generic, copy-paste letters that name no specific role. Reviewers spot them immediately and read them as a sign the partnership is not real.
