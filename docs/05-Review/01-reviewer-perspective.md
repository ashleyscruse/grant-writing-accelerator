# The Reviewer's Perspective

Stage 5 has two parts. This page covers the first: reading your own proposal the way a reviewer will. The next page covers compliance and submission. Do not skip either, and do not submit until you have done both. A strong proposal that breaks a formatting rule gets returned without review.

The goal here is simple. Before a stranger scores your draft, you score it yourself, honestly, against the same rubric they will use.

## How reviewers actually read

Reviewers are usually exhausted, overworked experts reading proposals outside their immediate expertise, often late at night. They have roughly five minutes to summarize your aims, approach, and significance to the rest of the panel. That reality shapes everything about how you should write and review.

What it means for your draft:

- If the reviewer cannot quickly state your project's story in plain terms, the proposal scores poorly.
- The first page (or the Specific Aims page for NIH) is often the only part every reviewer reads in full.
- Reviewers read with a pencil, hunting for reasons to say yes or no.
- Dense, poorly organized text gets skimmed, not read.
- Reviewers may read sections out of order, or read only certain parts.

They are not reading for pleasure. They are scoring against criteria and looking for risk.

## What reviewers reward

| What they want | What it looks like in your draft |
|---|---|
| Low risk, high return | A clear problem, an approach with evidence, a capable team, scope matched to budget and timeline |
| Clarity of purpose | A problem you can state in one sentence, a logical thread from need to method to outcome |
| Timeliness | Why this matters now, what changed, what happens if it is not funded |
| Feasibility | Preliminary data, realistic milestones, credentialed personnel with committed time, supporting infrastructure |
| Impact | Immediate deliverables plus long-term significance, and potential to replicate or scale |

## Red flags that tank scores

Read your draft looking for each of these. If you find one, fix it before submission.

1. Vague or unmeasurable objectives ("increase awareness" with no measure).
2. Budget and narrative mismatch: an activity with no budget line, or a budget line with no narrative.
3. Generic letters of support that do not name the project or the partner's role.
4. No preliminary data, no pilot, no theoretical basis.
5. Poor writing: typos, grammatical errors, passive voice, inconsistent terminology.
6. No discussion of risks, limitations, or mitigation.
7. A solution looking for a problem the target population does not actually have.
8. Overpromising outcomes the scope, budget, and timeline cannot deliver.
9. Internal inconsistency: goals that contradict methods, or numbers that do not match across sections.
10. Noncompliance: over the page limit, wrong font, missing sections, late.

## Common mistakes

**Process:** Not following the funder's instructions (the number one reason for rejection). Submitting the same text to every funder. Insufficient funder research. Submitting at the last minute. Skipping outside review.

**Content:** Centering your organization's needs instead of the community's. Confusing outputs (what you do) with outcomes (what changes). Goals that are not SMART. Personnel with no defined role. Measuring activities instead of change. Ignoring sustainability. No data behind impact claims. Deficit-based language about the community.

**Writing:** Excessive passive voice. Jargon with no explanation for mixed-discipline reviewers. Long sentences and paragraphs. No visual hierarchy. Inconsistent terminology. Emotional or unsupported claims.

## The scanability test

Reviewers scan before they read, so design the document so the key points are visible at a glance.

Apply these to every major section:

- Use headers that match the funder's evaluation criteria.
- Bold the critical words and findings, not whole sentences.
- Use bulleted and numbered lists for complex information.
- Use figures, tables, and timelines to summarize data.
- Leave white space around headers and key sections.
- Keep paragraphs to three to five sentences.
- Lead each paragraph with a topic sentence that states the point, then give evidence.

**The one-page test.** For each major section, read only the first sentence of every paragraph plus the bold text. Do you still get the full picture? If not, restructure until you do.

**Acronym discipline.** Spell out every acronym on first use in every major section, since reviewers may not read in order. Do not assume they know field abbreviations. Limit the total count.

## Use AI as a skeptical reviewer

The fastest way to catch what a reviewer would mark down is to ask the AI to act as one. Give it your draft and the solicitation, then tell it to score, not to praise. Ask for the marks against you, the gaps, and the specific lines that would lose points.

The AI critiques against the rubric and flags red flags, gaps, and inconsistencies. You make the final calls on what to cut, strengthen, or reframe.

### Example prompts

Score against the criteria:

> Act as a skeptical reviewer on this panel. Score this draft against the solicitation's review criteria, criterion by criterion. For each one, give the score you would assign, the single biggest weakness, and the exact text a reviewer would mark down.

Run the scanability test:

> For each major section, read only the headers, bold text, and first sentence of each paragraph. Tell me whether a five-minute reviewer would grasp the aims, approach, and significance from that alone. Flag every section that fails.

Hunt for inconsistency:

> Check this proposal for internal inconsistency. List every place where a number, date, dollar amount, or key term does not match across sections. Flag any acronym used before it is defined in a given section.

Find the red flags:

> Review this draft against the ten reviewer red flags: vague objectives, budget-narrative mismatch, generic letters, no preliminary data, weak writing, no risk discussion, solution-looking-for-a-problem, overpromising, internal inconsistency, and noncompliance. Tell me which apply and quote the offending text.

Pressure-test feasibility:

> A reviewer's job is to find reasons to say no. Give me the three strongest feasibility objections a reviewer would raise about this scope, budget, and timeline, and tell me where the draft fails to preempt them.

Run these passes, fix what they surface, then move to the compliance and submission check. You do not submit until both are clean.
