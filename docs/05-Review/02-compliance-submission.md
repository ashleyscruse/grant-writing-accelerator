# Compliance and Submission

This is the second half of Stage 5. The reviewer-perspective pass tells you whether the proposal is good. The compliance pass tells you whether it is allowed. Both have to pass.

The hard line holds here: do not submit until you have read the proposal as a reviewer and checked it against the solicitation's compliance rules. A strong proposal that violates a formatting requirement gets returned without review. Noncompliance is the cheapest way to lose, and the easiest to prevent.

## The compliance pass

Work straight from the solicitation, not from memory. Pull the exact requirements and check the draft against each one.

### Formatting and structure

| Requirement | What to verify |
|---|---|
| Page limits | Each section is within its limit, and the full proposal is within the total |
| Font | Typeface and point size match the solicitation exactly (often a named font and minimum size) |
| Margins | Meet the stated minimum on all sides |
| Line spacing | Matches the rule (single, 1.5, or a stated maximum lines per inch) |
| Required components | Every required document is present (narrative, budget, biosketches, letters, data management plan, and any others named) |
| Section headers | Match the funder's criteria or the RFP structure |
| File format and naming | PDF or the specified format, with the required file names |
| Other formatting | Figures, references, page numbers, and any solicitation-specific rules |

### Narrative and budget alignment

- Every activity in the narrative has a corresponding budget line.
- Every budget line connects back to an activity in the narrative.
- The budget narrative justifies each cost and ties it to the work.
- Personnel effort in the budget matches the roles and time described in the narrative.

### Internal consistency

- Numbers, dates, and dollar amounts match across every section.
- The total in the budget matches the total in the narrative and any summary.
- Goals, methods, evaluation, and budget tell one coherent story.
- Each acronym is spelled out on first use in every major section, and terminology is consistent throughout.

## Pre-submission checklist

Run this after the compliance pass and before you touch the submission portal.

- [ ] Every evaluation criterion is addressed explicitly in the narrative.
- [ ] Page limits met for each section and the whole proposal.
- [ ] Font, margins, and spacing match the solicitation.
- [ ] All required components are present and complete.
- [ ] File format and file names match the instructions.
- [ ] Narrative and budget align in both directions.
- [ ] Budget totals match across all sections.
- [ ] Numbers, dates, and dollar amounts are consistent throughout.
- [ ] Acronyms are defined on first use in each major section.
- [ ] Letters of support are specific, signed, and reference the project.
- [ ] Biosketches and personnel documents use the required format.
- [ ] At least one colleague has reviewed the full package.
- [ ] All registrations are active and current.
- [ ] Sponsored programs office has reviewed and signed off.
- [ ] Internal deadline met, with buffer before the funder deadline.

## Submission logistics

Submission is rarely a single button. For federal proposals especially, the system registrations take days or weeks, so confirm them early, not on deadline day.

### Registrations that gate submission

| System | When it applies |
|---|---|
| SAM.gov | Required for the organization to receive federal funds; renewals can take time, so verify it is active |
| Grants.gov | Common federal submission portal across many agencies |
| Research.gov | NSF submission system |
| eRA Commons | NIH and several other HHS agency submissions; principal investigators need an active account and role |

Confirm which systems this solicitation uses, that your organization and your own accounts are registered and active, and that your role permissions allow submission well before the deadline.

### Deadlines and routing

- Most institutions submit through a sponsored programs office, not the individual investigator. The office, not you, often pushes the final button.
- That office sets an internal deadline ahead of the funder deadline, frequently several business days earlier. Treat the internal deadline as the real one.
- Build in buffer. Portals slow down and crash near deadlines, and last-minute submission raises error rates.
- Know whether the deadline is a date or a specific time, and in which time zone.
- Route the package for institutional review and signatures with enough lead time to fix anything they flag.

## How AI helps, and where it stops

AI is strong at the checking work in this stage. Point it at the solicitation and the draft and have it verify:

- Page, font, margin, and spacing rules against the actual requirements.
- That every required component is present.
- Narrative-to-budget alignment in both directions.
- Consistency of numbers, dates, totals, and acronyms across sections.

Example prompt:

> Using this solicitation, build a compliance checklist of every formatting and required-component rule, then check my draft against each item and tell me exactly which ones fail and where.

AI cannot do the rest. It cannot confirm your SAM.gov or eRA Commons registration is active, cannot route the package through your sponsored programs office, cannot collect signatures, and cannot submit on your behalf. Those steps are yours, and they run on real-world clocks. Start them early.

You make the final calls on what to cut, strengthen, or reframe, and you submit. The deliverable for Stage 5 is a review-ready, compliant draft and a submission plan you have actually executed.
