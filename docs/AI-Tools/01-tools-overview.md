# Tools That See Your Files

Most people use AI for grant writing like this: open a chat window in the browser, paste in a chunk of your draft or a solicitation, read the response, copy it back into your document, and repeat. It works, but it is slow, and the AI is working blind. It cannot see your profile, your past grants, your budget, or the rest of the proposal. So it writes for a researcher who does not exist, a plausible average, and you spend your time deleting filler and re-explaining yourself.

File-aware tools change that. They run where your files are and read them directly. The AI sees your profile and your draft, and it edits in place. No copy. No paste. You stop being a courier between two windows.

## Why file access matters for grant work

Your proposal is not one document. It is a profile, a project concept, several narrative sections, a budget justification, a facilities section, and a stack of solicitation requirements. A tool that can read all of that at once can keep your aims consistent, pull real details from your profile into a biosketch, check a section against the solicitation, and draft directly into the file you are working in. A chat window that only sees what you pasted cannot do any of that.

All of the file-aware tools below can read the starter workspace, the folder of markdown that holds your profile, your skills, and your grants. Point any of them at that folder and the AI starts from who you actually are.

## The tools

| Tool | What it is | Best for in grant work |
|---|---|---|
| **Claude Code** | CLI agent in your terminal with full access to your files, commands, and search | Drafting and revising sections across many files at once, pulling profile details into a biosketch, checking a draft against a solicitation, building your whole proposal in place |
| **Cursor** | AI-powered code and text editor (a VS Code fork) with inline editing and full project context | Editing a section interactively, highlight a paragraph and rewrite it, reference another file with @filename to keep aims consistent |
| **VS Code + Copilot** | The widely used editor with GitHub Copilot built in | If you already work in VS Code, AI help with your markdown drafts and budget tables without switching tools |
| **Antigravity (Google)** | Google's agent-first IDE, a VS Code fork powered by Gemini with multi-agent orchestration | Larger proposals where you want several drafting and review passes running in parallel across sections |
| **Claude desktop app / Claude on the web (Projects)** | Claude with a Project: upload your profile and solicitation once, and it stays in context across the conversation | Long-form drafting and editing when you prefer a chat interface, with your documents loaded so it is not starting from zero |
| **Perplexity** | Search-grounded AI that cites sources | Finding funding opportunities, programs, and background facts; quick "who funds this and when is it due" lookups |
| **Gemini Deep Research** | Long-context research that synthesizes many sources into a report | Deeper scans of a funding landscape, a program's priorities, or prior awards before you commit to an opportunity |

## How to think about the split

The first four tools (Claude Code, Cursor, VS Code + Copilot, Antigravity) live in your editor and see your whole workspace. Reach for them when you are drafting, revising, and checking the proposal itself.

The Claude Projects option sits in the middle: a chat interface, but with your documents loaded so it works from your real context.

Perplexity and Gemini Deep Research are for the front of the process: finding the opportunity, understanding the funder, and gathering background. They do not edit your files, but they are the fastest way to answer "what should I even apply to?"

You do not need all of them. Pick one file-aware tool to draft in, and one research tool to find opportunities. That is enough to move faster than copy-paste from day one.

## The rule

AI drafts text. AI finds opportunities. AI checks your draft against the rules.

**You** decide what to apply for. **You** own the science and the claims. **You** sign the proposal.

The tools do not write the grant. You write the grant, faster.
